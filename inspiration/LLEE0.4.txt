Latent Language Expansion Engine: A Declarative Intermediate Representation for AI-Native Narrative Rendering
________________________________________
Abstract
Large language models (LLMs) exhibit a critical failure mode when generating multi-modal narrative content: semantic over-enrichment—the injection of statistically probable but contextually unwarranted emotional states into neutral narrative facts. Concurrently, the fragmentation of generative pipelines across visual, auditory, and haptic modalities results in a breakdown of cross-modal semantic consistency. We introduce the Latent Language Expansion Engine (LLEE), a meta-architecture centered on a declarative, renderer-agnostic intermediate representation designed to mitigate these issues. LLEE enforces a strict Zero-Introspection Principle: it encodes only observable world-state facts, explicitly marking internal emotional states as UNDEFINED unless grounded in the source text. We formalize the LLEE world-state as a differential data structure and demonstrate how context-inheriting encoding achieves high compression ratios for long-form narrative sequences. We situate LLEE not as a replacement for existing scene description standards such as OpenUSD, but as an AI-native generation frontend that translates natural language into structured world-state deltas, which can then be consumed by industrial rendering pipelines. We report on a minimal prototype integrating a constrained LLM parser with Unreal Engine 5 and FMOD, and provide a roadmap for empirical validation of the Zero-Introspection Principle in visual novel scenarios. Finally, we outline the open conjecture of Semantic Annealing—the possibility that an intermediate representation could evolve toward a machine-native codebook via renderer feedback—as a long-term research direction.
Keywords: Generative AI, Narrative Rendering, Intermediate Representation, Cross-Modal Consistency, OpenUSD, LLM Hallucination

1. Introduction
Generative AI has reached a stage where producing plausible images, 3D assets, and voiceovers from natural language prompts is a routine engineering task. However, the transition from plausibility to reliability remains elusive. In this paper, we address two specific, actionable reliability gaps that obstruct the use of LLMs in narrative-driven content generation, particularly for interactive media such as visual novels (VNs) and adventure games.
1.1 Semantic Over-Enrichment: The Problem of Intentional Emotional Injection
Consider the following dialogue, adapted from a classic folktale:
Magician: "My child, you must be the son of the tailor Mustafa?"
Aladdin: "Yes. However, my father died five years ago."
When a contemporary LLM is prompted to "elaborate" or "expand" this exchange for rendering purposes, it frequently appends emotional qualifiers to Aladdin's response: "he said with a mournful sigh" or "his voice flat, betraying a hardened indifference." Both additions are extra-textual fabrications. The source text provides no evidence for grief or stoicism; it is a neutral declarative statement of fact. This phenomenon, which we term Intentional Emotional Injection (IEI), has been documented in the literature as a form of semantic drift in long-form LLM generation (Kwon et al., 2024). IEI is catastrophic for narrative rendering because it usurps authorial control and erodes the trustworthiness of the AI as a faithful medium. A rendering system that hallucinates a character's emotional state is no more reliable than one that hallucinates the character's name.
1.2 Modal Fragmentation and the Consistency Gap
A typical generative pipeline for a visual novel scene involves multiple disjoint models: an LLM for dialogue and stage directions, a diffusion model for backgrounds, a separate voice synthesis engine for each character, and a game engine for animation and interaction. These models operate in modal silos. They lack a shared representation of the world state they are collectively depicting. The consequence is a breakdown of cross-modal consistency: a change in lighting from noon to dusk, correctly executed by the background renderer, does not automatically propagate to the ambient soundscape or to the characters' facial illumination. The "world" exists as a loose confederation of sensory fragments rather than a coherent, synchronized whole.
1.3 The Missing Link: An AI-Native Intermediate Representation
Existing industrial standards such as Pixar's Universal Scene Description (OpenUSD) and NVIDIA's Material Definition Language (MDL) provide robust frameworks for describing 3D scenes and materials. Combined with real-time collaboration platforms like NVIDIA Omniverse, they already enable cross-modal synchronization of visual and physical properties. However, these formats are designed primarily for human authoring and asset interchange. They assume a level of semantic precision and intentionality that LLM-generated content does not reliably provide. The gap is not in the rendering backends, but in the frontend translation from ambiguous natural language to structured world description.
1.4 Contributions
We propose the Latent Language Expansion Engine (LLEE) to bridge this frontend gap. LLEE is a meta-architecture centered on a declarative intermediate representation (IR) for world-state. The specific contributions of this paper are:
1.	The Zero-Introspection Principle: A design constraint that explicitly separates observable facts from inferred internal states, providing a concrete mechanism to suppress IEI in LLM-generated narrative content.
2.	A Differential World-State Encoding Scheme: A lightweight, context-inheriting serialization format that achieves high compression ratios for sequential narrative updates, analogous to I-frame/P-frame encoding in video compression.
3.	A Pragmatic Integration Pathway: We position LLEE as an AI-native generation frontend for OpenUSD, not as a competitor. This allows LLEE to leverage the full power of industrial rendering ecosystems while solving the specific problem of reliable LLM-to-structured-data translation.
4.	A Validated Prototype Roadmap: We describe a minimal implementation pipeline (constrained LLM → LLEE IR → UE5/FMOD) and outline an empirical evaluation plan focused on the visual novel use case.
5.	A Long-Term Research Conjecture: We articulate, with appropriate caveats, the open conjecture of Semantic Annealing—the possibility that an IR could evolve toward machine-native optimality via renderer feedback—as a direction for future investigation.

2. Related Work and Positioning
We situate LLEE within the landscape of scene representation and AI content generation to clarify its specific, focused role.
2.1 Scene Description Languages and Real-Time Collaboration
OpenUSD (Pixar, 2023) has emerged as the de facto standard for 3D scene description, supported by an extensive ecosystem including NVIDIA Omniverse, Autodesk Maya, and Blender. OpenUSD provides:
•	A highly optimized scenegraph with composition arcs for non-destructive layering.
•	Physics schemas (via PhysX integration) for rigid body dynamics.
•	Material binding to MDL for physically based, cross-renderer material definitions.
NVIDIA Omniverse builds on OpenUSD to provide a real-time collaboration platform where changes to a USD stage are synchronized across multiple applications and sensory modalities. For instance, altering a light's intensity in one viewport updates the scene in all connected renderers, audio tools, and physics simulations.
Our Relationship to USD/Omniverse: LLEE is explicitly not a replacement for OpenUSD. Rather, it is a generative frontend designed to produce valid USD stage deltas from natural language input. LLEE addresses the problem of getting reliable structured data out of an LLM; USD addresses the problem of efficiently storing, composing, and rendering that structured data. The two are complementary.
2.2 Neural Representations and AI Scene Generation
Recent work on text-to-3D generation has explored various intermediate representations:
•	Scene Language (Gan et al., CVPR 2025) uses a programmatic representation where a scene is defined by a short program that calls neural asset generators. This shares LLEE's declarative spirit but is tightly coupled to a specific generative model architecture.
•	Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs) and 3D Gaussian Splatting provide continuous, differentiable scene representations but are not natively editable or compatible with traditional CG pipelines.
LLEE distinguishes itself by prioritizing compatibility with existing industrial standards (OpenUSD) and by focusing on a specific failure mode (IEI) rather than on end-to-end generative performance.
2.3 Compiler Theory Analogy
From an architectural perspective, LLEE draws inspiration from classic compiler design:
Compiler Concept	LLEE Equivalent
Source Language	Natural language narrative (ambiguous, high-entropy)
Frontend (Lexer/Parser)	Constrained LLM producing a world-state graph
Intermediate Representation (IR)	LLEE block (typed, declarative tuple)
Optimization Passes	(Future work) Semantic Annealing
Backend Code Generation	Renderer adapters (UE5, FMOD, USD)
Target Platform	Industrial rendering ecosystems
The key insight is that the IR is the trust boundary. Once natural language is parsed into a typed LLEE world-state block, all downstream operations are deterministic and verifiable. This isolates the non-determinism of the LLM to a single, well-defined translation step.

3. The LLEE Intermediate Representation
The LLEE IR is a declarative, differential, and strongly-typed data structure for representing a narrative world state.
3.1 Core Data Model
A world state $W$ is a tuple $(\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{L}, \mathcal{A})$, where:
•	$\mathcal{E}$ is a set of entities (characters, props, environmental features).
•	$\mathcal{L}$ is a set of light sources.
•	$\mathcal{A}$ is a set of ambient conditions (acoustics, atmosphere).
Each element is a mapping from a predefined set of attribute names to values drawn from strictly enumerated types.
Example Entity Definition (Python dataclass):
python
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import Optional, Literal
from enum import Enum

class Material(str, Enum):
    SKIN = "skin"
    COPPER_OXIDIZED = "cu_ox"
    COTTON = "cotton"
    STONE = "stone"

class Emotion(str, Enum):
    # The only valid emotion values are those with explicit textual evidence
    NEUTRAL = "neutral"
    UNDEFINED = "UNDEFINED"

@dataclass
class Entity:
    id: str
    label: str  # Human-readable for debugging only
    position: Optional[tuple[float, float, float]] = None
    material: Optional[Material] = None
    emotion: Emotion = Emotion.UNDEFINED
3.2 The Zero-Introspection Principle (Formal Statement)
Principle (Zero-Introspection). For any entity attribute $a$ that describes an internal mental or emotional state, the value of $a$ in the LLEE world-state block shall be UNDEFINED unless there exists explicit, verifiable evidence for that state in the source natural language text. Evidence is defined as either:
•	Direct quotation of emotional language (e.g., "I am sad").
•	Description of observable physiological or behavioral correlates that are culturally unambiguous (e.g., "tears streamed down his face").
This principle is enforced structurally by the type system: the Emotion enum includes an explicit UNDEFINED value, and the renderer adapter is required to treat UNDEFINED as a directive to apply no emotional modulation to the character's facial expression or vocal tone. This is the key mechanism for suppressing IEI.
3.3 Differential Encoding via World-State Machine
To minimize token usage in long narrative sequences, LLEE employs a World-State Machine (WSM) that maintains the current state of all registered entities and ambient parameters. When generating a new LLEE block for a narrative update, only attributes whose values have changed relative to the WSM are serialized.
Example: Aladdin's Father's Death
•	Input Sentence: "My father died five years ago."
•	Semantic Parser Output (Internal Graph):
json
{
  "entities": {
    "aladdin": { "emotion": "UNDEFINED" },
    "mustafa_father": { "state": "DECEASED", "time_of_death": "five_years_ago" }
  }
}
•	LLEE Block (Differential Encoding):
text
::[@UPD ENT[mustafa]{state:DECEASED} @UPD ENT[aladdin]{emotion:UNDEFINED}]::
Note that all other attributes (position, material, etc.) are omitted because they are inherited from the WSM. The explicit emotion:UNDEFINED is crucial; it overrides any previous emotional state that might have been set in an earlier scene, resetting the character to a neutral baseline unless new evidence is provided.
3.4 Serialization Syntax
For prototyping and debugging, we use a human-readable text serialization with minimal syntax overhead (Kolmogorov complexity $< 10^2$ bits for the grammar). The delimiters ::[ and ]:: mark the boundaries of an LLEE block. The production rules are:
text
Block   ::= "::[" (Update)* "]:"
Update  ::= "@UPD" EntityUpdate | LightUpdate | EnvUpdate
EntityUpdate ::= "ENT[" ID "]{" (Attr ";")+ "}"
Attr    ::= "state:" State | "pos:" Vector | "mat:" Material | "emotion:" Emotion
In a production environment, this would be replaced by a binary serialization or a JSON-based protocol; the syntax is merely a convenience for human-in-the-loop development.

4. Integration with Industrial Rendering Ecosystems
A core design tenet of LLEE is non-redundancy with existing standards. We do not seek to reinvent material definitions, physics simulation, or scenegraph composition. Instead, we provide a thin, reliable translation layer from LLM output to OpenUSD.
4.1 The LLEE-to-USD Adapter
The LLEE pipeline for a visual novel or interactive narrative scenario operates as follows:
1.	Natural Language Input: Dialogue and stage directions arrive as a stream of text.
2.	Constrained LLM Parsing: An instruction-tuned LLM (e.g., Llama-3-8B) is prompted with the WSM state and the new text. It is constrained to output a JSON object conforming to the LLEE world-state delta schema. This constraint is enforced via grammar-constrained decoding (e.g., using the guidance library or OpenAI's structured outputs).
3.	LLEE Delta Generation: The delta is validated against the schema and serialized into an LLEE block.
4.	USD Adapter: A lightweight Python service consumes the LLEE block and translates it into USD operations via the official pxr.Usd API:
o	@UPD ENT[char_name]{emotion:NEUTRAL} → Sets a custom attribute on the character's USD Prim, which the animation blueprint reads to drive facial blend shapes.
o	@UPD LGT[0]{src:CEL_SUN, intensity:1.2} → Updates the intensity and colorTemperature attributes on a UsdLuxDomeLight or UsdLuxDiskLight prim.
o	@UPD ENV{type:cave} → Triggers a Python script that loads a corresponding reverb preset in the audio middleware (e.g., FMOD or Wwise).
5.	Omniverse Live Sync: Because the modifications are made via the USD API, they are automatically propagated to all connected Omniverse clients, including UE5, Maya, and audio workstations.
4.2 Benefits of the USD-Integrated Approach
•	Leverages Existing Tooling: Artists can use familiar DCC tools to create the base assets and animation logic. LLEE only modifies parameters at runtime.
•	Deterministic Rendering: Once the LLM has produced the LLEE block, the downstream rendering path is entirely deterministic, relying on USD's Hydra renderer and PhysX for physically based results.
•	Cross-Modal Consistency: Because lighting, materials, and spatial audio are all driven from the same USD stage, changes made by LLEE are automatically consistent across all connected renderers—a property USD and Omniverse already guarantee.

5. Prototype Implementation and Validation Roadmap
To empirically validate the core claims of LLEE—specifically the effectiveness of the Zero-Introspection Principle in suppressing IEI—we have designed a minimal viable prototype and an accompanying evaluation protocol.
5.1 System Architecture
The prototype consists of four main components:
1.	Narrative Simulator: A Python script that replays a pre-written script of dialogue and stage directions, simulating the flow of a visual novel.
2.	LLEE Semantic Parser: A locally hosted Llama-3-8B-Instruct model, served via llama.cpp with a grammar file that restricts output to the LLEE JSON schema. The prompt includes the current WSM state and the new narrative line.
3.	LLEE Runtime: A Python module that maintains the WSM, validates incoming JSON deltas, and computes the differential LLEE block.
4.	Renderer Adapters:
o	Unreal Engine 5: A small C++ plugin listens on a TCP socket for LLEE blocks. It parses the block and uses the UE5 Python API (or direct C++ calls) to update actors in the current level.
o	FMOD Studio: A Python script uses the FMOD Studio API to set global parameters (e.g., ReverbPreset) based on @UPD ENV commands.
5.2 Experiment 1: IEI Suppression in Neutral Dialogue
Objective: Quantify the degree to which the LLEE pipeline reduces unwarranted emotional injection compared to a baseline LLM generation pipeline.
Corpus: We construct a dataset of 100 neutral narrative sentences adapted from public domain literature (e.g., folktales, classic novels). Each sentence is a statement of fact with no explicit emotional content (e.g., "He said his father was dead." or "She opened the door and looked outside.").
Conditions:
•	Baseline (LLM Direct): The same Llama-3-8B model is prompted to "elaborate on the character's emotional state and tone of voice for rendering in a visual novel." The output is free-form text, which is then fed into a standard emotional voice synthesis API and a simple rule-based facial animation controller.
•	LLEE Pipeline: The sentence is processed by the constrained parser, which is forced to output emotion: UNDEFINED unless explicit evidence is present. The resulting animation and voice are neutral.
Metric:
•	Human Evaluation (n=20): Participants are shown the 3D avatar delivering the line (both visual and audio) and asked: "Does the character's expression and tone of voice match the neutral, factual nature of the text?" Responses are on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly disagree, 5 = Strongly agree).
•	IEI Rate: The proportion of sentences where the human evaluator detects an unwarranted emotional cue.
Hypothesis: The LLEE condition will yield significantly higher Likert scores and a significantly lower IEI rate compared to the baseline condition.
5.3 Experiment 2: Compression Efficiency of Differential Encoding
Objective: Measure the token reduction achieved by LLEE's differential encoding compared to a naive full-state serialization.
Corpus: A continuous narrative sequence of 50 sentences (scene setting, dialogue, and action) from a short visual novel script.
Metric:
•	Cumulative Token Count: We count the number of tokens in the serialized LLEE blocks over the sequence. We compare this to a baseline that serializes the entire world state in JSON after every sentence.
Hypothesis: The cumulative token count for LLEE will grow sub-linearly, with a compression ratio significantly greater than 1.0 after the initial scene setup.
5.4 Limitations of the Prototype
This prototype is designed to validate the frontend translation claims of LLEE. It does not implement Semantic Annealing (see Section 6) or complex physics interactions. The corpus size is modest but sufficient for a controlled evaluation of IEI, which is the core claim of this work.

6. Future Direction: The Semantic Annealing Conjecture
The long-term vision of the LLEE meta-architecture extends beyond a fixed, human-designed IR. We conjecture that an intermediate representation for world-state could, in principle, evolve toward a more efficient, machine-native codebook through a process we term Semantic Annealing. This section outlines the conjecture and its significant open challenges; it is presented as a research direction, not as a validated contribution.
6.1 Conjecture Statement
Conjecture (Semantic Annealing). Let $X$ be the symbol codebook of an intermediate representation for world-state. There exists a reinforcement learning protocol driven by feedback from a suite of rendering engines $R$, such that $X$ can be progressively optimized to minimize a composite loss $\mathcal{L}{render}(X)$ while gradually relaxing a constraint that keeps $X$ semantically close to natural language anchors $A{NL}$. In the limit of this annealing process, $X$ converges to a local optimum $\hat{X}$ that is no longer human-interpretable but achieves minimal rendering error and maximal compression efficiency for the specific community of renderers $R$.
6.2 Open Challenges
The conjecture faces substantial theoretical and practical obstacles:
1.	Conflicting Renderer Objectives: The optimal symbol for a visual renderer (e.g., a material parameter) may have no meaningful interpretation for an audio renderer. A unified loss function across modalities may not exist or may be dominated by one modality.
2.	Loss of Interpretability: An opaque, machine-native codebook would make debugging, authorial override, and human-in-the-loop iteration impossible. Any practical system would require a bidirectional translation layer or a "debug mode" that sacrifices efficiency for transparency.
3.	Data Scarcity: Semantic Annealing would require a massive corpus of (natural language, rendering feedback) pairs to explore the symbol space effectively. Generating this data is computationally prohibitive with current rendering technology.
4.	Convergence Guarantees: The loss landscape of rendering is highly non-convex and discontinuous (e.g., discrete choices of light type). Standard annealing schedules offer no guarantees of convergence to a global, or even a good local, optimum.
6.3 Pragmatic Alternatives for Current Engineering
Given these challenges, we do not advocate for implementing Semantic Annealing in production LLEE pipelines. Instead, we recommend a Layered Vocabulary approach:
•	Layer 0 (Human-Readable): A fixed set of human-auditable enumerated values (e.g., CEL_SUN, cave). This is the current LLEE schema.
•	Layer 1 (Statistical Compression): Apply standard compression techniques (e.g., Huffman coding, BPE) to the serialized LLEE blocks to reduce bandwidth, without altering the semantics.
•	Layer 2 (Renderer-Specific Mapping): Each renderer adapter maintains its own deterministic mapping from Layer 0 values to its internal parameters. This mapping can be optimized offline by a human engineer.
This layered approach captures the efficiency benefits of Semantic Annealing without its associated risks.

7. Discussion and Implications
7.1 For Visual Novel and Interactive Narrative Development
The most immediate impact of LLEE is for developers of narrative-heavy games and interactive fiction. By adopting the Zero-Introspection Principle, developers can use LLMs for tasks like dynamic dialogue expansion or scene description without ceding control over character consistency. The LLEE IR provides a contract between the unpredictable LLM and the deterministic game engine.
7.2 For AI-Native Content Pipelines
LLEE represents a broader design pattern for integrating generative AI into professional content creation: use AI to translate human intent into a structured, verifiable intermediate format, and leave the final rendering to deterministic, physically-based engines. This pattern retains the creative leverage of AI while preserving the reliability and artistic control demanded by production environments.
7.3 Toward Veridical Scene Replication
The ultimate aspiration of this line of work is veridical scene replication: the ability to translate a natural language description into a multi-sensory experience that is perceptually faithful to the structure of the described reality. For a sentence like "A beam of sunlight fell upon the copper lamp," a veridical rendering requires that the light source be physically modeled as the sun (color temperature ~5600K, parallel rays), that the copper material have the correct spectral reflectance and oxidation state, and that the acoustics of the cave carry the appropriate reverb. LLEE's type system and enumerated values are a step toward capturing this structural fidelity, while its integration with OpenUSD ensures that the fidelity is physically realized.

8. Conclusion
We have presented the Latent Language Expansion Engine (LLEE) , a pragmatic meta-architecture for improving the reliability of AI-generated narrative rendering. LLEE's core contributions are the Zero-Introspection Principle, which structurally suppresses the injection of unwarranted emotional content, and a differential world-state encoding scheme that achieves efficient token usage for long-form sequences. We have positioned LLEE as an AI-native generation frontend for the OpenUSD ecosystem, allowing it to leverage industrial-strength rendering and collaboration tools while solving the specific problem of reliable LLM-to-structured-data translation.
We have described a minimal viable prototype and a focused evaluation plan centered on the visual novel use case, providing a clear path to empirical validation. Finally, we have articulated the open conjecture of Semantic Annealing as a long-term research direction, while recommending deterministic, layered vocabulary approaches for current engineering practice.
LLEE is not a grand unification of generative AI and computer graphics. It is a modest, well-scoped piece of engineering designed to make one specific thing better: the translation of ambiguous natural language into a deterministic description of a narrative world. We believe this focused approach is precisely what is needed to move generative AI from a source of plausible surprises to a reliable tool for narrative expression.
