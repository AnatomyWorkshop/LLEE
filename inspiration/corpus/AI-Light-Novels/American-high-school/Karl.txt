The name "Karl" hangs in the stale, air-conditioned space of the classroom. It’s not a question, but a summons. Your name, spoken by Mr. Harris, cuts through the low hum of adolescent indifference. The sound of it feels foreign, too sharp for the drowsy Monday morning atmosphere.

You feel a dozen pairs of eyes, previously glazed over or locked onto phone screens now hidden in laps, shift towards you. They aren't hostile, not yet. They are simply curious, like cows in a field turning to watch a car pass. You are the car. The sudden focal point in a landscape of worn-out desks and the faint, chalky smell of forgotten equations.

Your chair legs scrape against the linoleum floor, a gritty, complaining sound that feels amplified in the momentary quiet. You push yourself up, your body feeling strangely heavy. The worn denim of your jeans rustles. You can feel the slightly cool, smooth surface of the window glass near your left shoulder. Outside, the morning sun casts a pale, golden light on the empty school lawn, making the dew on the grass blades glitter. It looks peaceful, a world away from the subtle social calculus happening inside this room.

"Karl," you say. Your voice comes out steadier than you expected, a simple statement of fact.

Mr. Harris leans back further in his chair, the cheap pleather groaning in protest. He takes a slow sip from his enormous, dented thermos. The logo on it is so faded it’s just a smear of color. He doesn't rush you. He has all the time in the world.

"And," he prompts, his voice thick with a kind of practiced boredom, "your fun fact from the summer. Enlighten us. Try not to make it about Fortnite. I swear to God, if one more of you tells me you got a Victory Royale, I’m assigning a ten-page paper on the sociopolitical implications of the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act."

A few nervous chuckles ripple through the front rows. The jocks in the back remain silent, unimpressed. The threat is empty, but it serves its purpose: raise the stakes. Don't be boring.

Your mind goes blank. A vast, white, static-filled void where a "fun fact" should be. What happened this summer? The image of your father's face, tight and pale, flickers in your head. The crinkled edge of a DNA report on the kitchen table. The finality in his voice when he said he was cutting you off. Fun.

Panic, cold and sharp, pricks at the base of your skull. You can't say that. You can't say you spent the summer watching your family implode. You can’t say you spent most of it in your room, the blinds drawn, feeling like a ghost in your own home. What else is there? Video games? That's out. Lying? You could say you went to California, learned to surf. A quick glance at your pale arms and average build tells you that lie wouldn't survive a second of scrutiny.

The silence stretches. It's only been a few seconds, but it feels like an eternity. You can feel the weight of their collective gaze intensifying. You are failing the first, unspoken test of high school: the test of effortless self-presentation.

Instinct, or maybe desperation, takes over. Your right hand lifts, your fingers almost unconsciously pressing against your temple. The skin there is cool. You apply a slight, firm pressure.

The world fractures.

It’s not a gentle fade. It’s a violent, kaleidoscopic shattering. The classroom, Mr. Harris, the faces of your new classmates—they all splinter into a million shards of light and sound, then reassemble into fleeting, chaotic visions.

Vision A: The screen of a TV, the vibrant colors of Fortnite. Your own voice saying, "…spent the whole summer grinding V-bucks." The classroom is silent. Someone in the back snorts derisively. The blonde girl in the front, Ashley—your mind somehow supplies the name—doesn't even turn around. She’s tapping a perfectly manicured nail on her Hydro Flask. The vision evaporates in a puff of disinterest. Boring. Safe, but social death.

Vision B: A bright, sun-drenched beach. Your voice, sounding thin and unconvincing, says, "…tried surfing in Malibu." A ripple of laughter from the back corner. One of the jocks, a thick-necked guy with a buzz cut, mouths "bullshit" to his friend. Mr. Harris sighs, a long, weary exhalation of disappointment. Humiliation. Worse than boring.

The visions spin, a whirlwind of potential futures, each one a different shade of failure. The pressure in your head intensifies, a dull, throbbing ache beginning to build behind your eyes. Your stomach lurches. A cold sweat beads on your forehead. Just one more. Something different.

Vision C: A patch of dry, sun-baked grass in a suburban backyard. The focused, intense beam of a magnifying glass. An ant wriggles, then goes still. The beam moves. A dry leaf curls and blackens, a wisp of smoke rising. The camera of your mind pans out, showing the leaf is part of a large, meticulously cared-for rose bush. A small flame blossoms at the base. It grows, greedily consuming the dead leaves and then licking at the living ones. Panic. The smell of burning petals. Your voice, calm and detached, says, "…I accidentally set my dad's prize-winning rose bush on fire while trying to see if a magnifying glass could kill a fire ant."

The vision shatters. The response is a mosaic of reactions. A few suppressed snorts of laughter from the middle rows. The jocks in the back exchange confused but intrigued looks. And Ashley, the blonde, she actually turns her head slightly, her brilliant blue eyes catching yours for a fraction of a second. There’s no smile, just a flicker of something unreadable—curiosity? Amusement? Mr. Harris’s lips twitch. Just a tiny, almost imperceptible upward curl at the corner of his mouth before it’s gone. He takes another long slurp from his thermos.

The headache is real now, a sharp spike behind your right eye. You feel a wave of nausea. But you have your answer.

You lower your hand from your temple, blinking to clear the last of the phantom images. The classroom settles back into focus.

"I spent the summer conducting a series of small-scale physics experiments in my backyard," you begin, your tone measured and even, mimicking the voice from the vision. "One of them involved focused solar energy, a magnifying glass, and insect specimens."

You pause. You have their attention now. Even the girl doodling on her notebook has looked up.

"The experiment was a success, in that I confirmed the hypothesis that a magnifying glass can, in fact, terminate a fire ant," you continue, deadpan. "However, there was an unforeseen variable. An ancillary ignition of nearby desiccated foliage, which led to the rapid and unfortunate immolation of my dad's prize-winning rose bush."

Silence.

Then, it starts. A single, choked giggle from a girl with braces. Then another, louder laugh from a guy in a Thrasher hoodie. Soon, a wave of genuine, unforced laughter ripples through the room. It’s not mocking laughter. It's appreciative. It’s the kind of laughter that acknowledges a story well told.

You look to the back. The jocks aren't laughing, but they're not sneering either. The thick-necked one is whispering to his friend, a grudging smirk on his face. They're intrigued. You’re not a try-hard; you’re a weirdo, but an interesting one.

You glance at the front. Ashley is facing forward again, but you can see her shoulders shaking slightly with silent laughter.

Finally, you look at Mr. Harris. He sets his thermos down with a soft click. He clears his throat, and the faintest hint of a smile is still playing on his lips.

"Well, Karl," he says, his voice losing some of its earlier weariness. "In the future, I'd advise keeping your pyromaniacal tendencies out of the field of entomology. Thank you for not mentioning Fortnite. You may sit down."

You sink back into your chair, the scraping sound barely audible over the subsiding chuckles. The wave of nausea hits you again, and you swallow hard. Your head is pounding. You risked a level 2 side effect for a good first impression. You look out the window, at the peaceful, sunlit lawn, and wonder if it was worth it.

The bell shrieks, a piercing, electronic scream that signals the end of first period. The spell is broken. Chairs scrape, binders snap shut, and the room instantly fills with the buzz of chatter and movement as thirty bodies surge towards the door. The transition is instantaneous, a collective release of pent-up energy.

You remain seated for a moment, letting the worst of the dizziness pass. The cool glass of the window feels good against your arm. The room empties out quickly. Mr. Harris is already engrossed in his phone, probably scrolling through Facebook, the ten-page paper threat completely forgotten. Ashley walks past your desk, her vanilla-scented perfume trailing in her wake. She doesn't look at you, but you feel the briefest shift in the air as she passes.

The classroom is nearly empty now, save for you and a couple of students slowly packing their bags. The air smells of Axe body spray, cheap perfume, and the faint, sweet scent of a mango Juul pod being surreptitiously hit in the hallway. Your next class, according to the schedule folded in your pocket, is math, on the third floor. You have ten minutes to get there.

You push yourself up, your backpack feeling heavy on your shoulder. The hallway is a chaotic river of bodies, a swirling mass of Champion hoodies, Lululemon leggings, and Fjällräven Kånken backpacks. You step into the current, just another anonymous face in the crowd, but something feels different. You’re still an unknown, but you’re not a zero. You’re the kid who burned his dad's roses. It’s a start.
